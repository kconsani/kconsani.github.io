Professor of Mathematics

Caterina (Katia) Consani

Johns Hopkins University

My research lies at the interface of noncommutative geometry, algebraic geometry, arithmetic geometry, and number theory, with a particular emphasis on new geometric approaches to arithmetic and the Riemann Hypothesis.

Prior to joining Johns Hopkins in 2005, I held positions at the University of Toronto and MIT. I received the title of Dottore di Ricerca in Matematica from the Universities of Genoa and Turin in 1993 and the Ph.D. in Mathematics from the University of Chicago in 1996. I am a Fellow of the American Mathematical Society.

Current research

My research develops geometric frameworks for arithmetic by enlarging the notion of space beyond classical algebraic geometry. Central constructions include the Arithmetic Site, the Scaling Site, the Picard geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\), and the recent absolute \(\mathbb F_1\)-geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\).

Noncommutative arithmetic geometry
BC-system, adele class space, spectral realization.
Arithmetic and Scaling Sites
Topos-theoretic models for the Riemann sector.
Absolute geometry
Segal \(\Gamma\)-rings, spherical algebras, characteristic-free geometry.
Perfectoid and Weil geometry
Fargues--Fontaine curve, local Weil groups, arithmetic curves.
