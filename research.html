Research

Research Overview

My research is centered on the development of new geometric frameworks for arithmetic. A recurring theme is that many phenomena traditionally expressed in analytic or algebraic terms admit a deeper geometric interpretation once one enlarges the notion of space beyond the setting of classical algebraic geometry.

This perspective has led to the introduction of several new geometric structures, including the Arithmetic Site, the Scaling Site, the Picard geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\), and the recent absolute \(\mathbb F_1\)-geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\). Together, these constructions form part of a long-term program aimed at extending geometric methods from function fields to arithmetic schemes and ultimately toward the Riemann Hypothesis.

\[ (\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z)_{\mathbb F_1},\qquad \operatorname{Pic}(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z),\qquad \mathcal X_{C^\flat,\mathbf Q_p},\qquad E_p\simeq \mathbf C^\times/p^{\mathbf Z}. \]

Quantum statistical mechanics and arithmetic

Goal: understand how noncommutative spaces and quantum statistical mechanics encode arithmetic symmetries.

Noncommutative arithmetic geometry emerged in the early 1990s with the introduction of the Bost--Connes quantum statistical dynamical system, commonly known as the BC-system. This remarkable system exhibits the Riemann zeta function as its partition function, while its zero-temperature equilibrium states realize the global class field isomorphism for the rational number field \(\mathbf Q\). It established one of the first conceptual bridges between noncommutative geometry, quantum statistical mechanics, and arithmetic.

In a long-term collaboration with Alain Connes, this circle of ideas was extended through new connections with \(p\)-adic analysis, Witt vectors, and \(p\)-adic Hodge theory. Among the outcomes is the construction of an archimedean analogue of Fontaine's period rings, providing an arithmetic framework at the archimedean place that parallels several fundamental features of \(p\)-adic Hodge theory.

Cyclic homology and archimedean arithmetic

Goal: reinterpret archimedean factors of arithmetic \(L\)-functions cohomologically.

Another direction, also developed in collaboration with Alain Connes, concerns the arithmetic interpretation of the cyclic homology of algebraic varieties. The cyclic homology of a scheme provides a natural cohomological framework in which Serre's archimedean local factors of the Hasse--Weil \(L\)-functions of smooth projective varieties over number fields admit a geometric interpretation as regularized determinants.

This work establishes a direct connection between cyclic homology, noncommutative geometry, and the arithmetic of \(L\)-functions, extending to the archimedean setting several ideas that traditionally belong to étale and crystalline cohomology.

The Arithmetic Site and the Scaling Site

Goal: construct geometric models for the finite and full Riemann sectors of the adele class space.

The study of the adele class space of \(\mathbf Q\) naturally led to the problem of constructing an algebro-geometric model for one of the fundamental noncommutative spaces underlying the spectral interpretation of the Riemann zeta function. This led to the introduction of the Arithmetic Site, originally defined as a pair consisting of a Grothendieck topos together with a structure sheaf over the Boolean semifield \(\mathbf B\), the smallest idempotent semifield, or equivalently the smallest semifield of characteristic one.

The Arithmetic Site provides an algebro-geometric realization of the finite part of the Riemann sector of the adele class space, namely the quotient obtained after removing the archimedean component, while remaining compatible with the original analytic description introduced by Alain Connes.

The Arithmetic Site naturally admits an extension of scalars from \(\mathbf B\) to the tropical semifield of real numbers. The resulting geometric object is the Scaling Site. Unlike the Arithmetic Site, the Scaling Site recovers the full Riemann sector of the adele class space, including its archimedean component, and its points are canonically identified with the corresponding adelic quotient.

These constructions were originally developed within the setting of characteristic-one algebraic structures. This naturally led to a corresponding homological algebra for sheaves of idempotent modules over a topos, with the category of modules over \(\mathbf B\) playing the role of the characteristic-one analogue of the category of abelian groups.

Characteristic-free absolute algebraic geometry

Goal: develop an absolute geometric framework independent of any fixed characteristic.

A natural limitation of the original characteristic-one framework is the absence of a canonical morphism from the ring of integers \(\mathbf Z\) to the Boolean semifield \(\mathbf B\). This observation motivated the search for a genuinely characteristic-free approach to absolute algebraic geometry.

This led to the development of a categorical theory of modules and algebras over the sphere spectrum, based on Segal's theory of \(\Gamma\)-sets and spherical algebras. Within this framework, geometry is developed directly over the sphere spectrum, providing a common foundation for arithmetic geometry that no longer depends on characteristic-one algebraic structures, while preserving the geometric insights originating from the Arithmetic Site.

Arakelov geometry, Picard geometry and the adele class space

Goal: construct arithmetic analogues of the Picard, Jacobian and Riemann--Roch theories of algebraic curves.

These ideas also lead naturally to Arakelov geometry, where they yield a new form of the Riemann--Roch theorem based on the interpretation of the compactification of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\) as an arithmetic curve over the absolute sphere spectrum.

The geometric interpretation of the adele class space also leads to a natural extension of global class field theory. Just as Grothendieck's étale fundamental group extends Galois theory from fields to schemes, the adele class space, viewed as a covering of the Scaling Site, provides an analogous extension of the class field isomorphism for \(\mathbf Q\) to arithmetic schemes related to \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\).

A natural continuation of this viewpoint is the development of a Picard--Jacobian theory for the arithmetic curve \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\). In this framework, elements of the adele class space, and in particular of its Riemann sector, are described in terms of torsion-free rank-one abelian groups equipped with additional rigidifying data. This leads to Picard monoids associated with \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\) and with the completed arithmetic curve, and to a corresponding arithmetic Jacobian obtained by quotienting by the natural scaling action of \(\mathbf R_+^*\).

Absolute geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\)

Goal: unify the arithmetic topos, characteristic-free geometry, perfectoid fields, the Fargues--Fontaine curve and local Weil groups.

The developments described above culminate in recent work on the absolute geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\), where ideas originating from the Arithmetic Site, absolute algebraic geometry, and the Picard geometry of the arithmetic curve are unified within a single characteristic-free geometric framework.

The key point is to retain the underlying arithmetic topos and its geometry of points, while replacing the original characteristic-one structure sheaf by the characteristic-free spherical \(\mathbb F_1\)-algebra \(\mathbb F_1[T]\). This leads to a new \(\mathbb F_1\)-arithmetic site and to the absolute arithmetic curve \[ (\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z)_{\mathbb F_1}. \] A central role is played by an Abel--Jacobi morphism from \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\) to the Picard geometry of \(\operatorname{Spec}\mathbb Z\), assigning to a prime \(p\) the rank-one group \(\mathbb Z[1/p]\).

One of the principal results shows that, when the absolute curve is evaluated on an algebraically closed perfectoid field of characteristic \(p\), its moduli of local points recover Scholze's heuristic description of untilts modulo Frobenius. A further result extends this picture to arbitrary perfectoid fields of residue characteristic \(p\): the local geometry at \(p\) canonically reconstructs the tilt and identifies the corresponding quotient with the closed points of the Fargues--Fontaine curve \(\mathcal X_{C^\flat,\mathbf Q_p}\), together with its distinguished point.

The same framework also admits an archimedean realization. Evaluating the absolute curve over the complex numbers reveals that the local arithmetic naturally extends the Frobenius action to actions of the local Weil groups \(W_v\), simultaneously at the finite place \(p\) and at the archimedean place. The corresponding spaces of local points become natural \(W_v\)-torsors, from which classical geometric objects arise, including the complex Tate elliptic curve \(E_p\simeq\mathbf C^\times/p^{\mathbf Z}\), its natural real structure, and the periodic orbit \(C_p\) appearing in the adelic geometry of the Riemann zeta function.

Long-term objective

Taken together, these developments illustrate a common objective: to construct new geometric frameworks capable of encoding the arithmetic of number fields, with the long-term goal of adapting and extending the geometric ideas underlying Weil's proof of the Riemann Hypothesis for function fields to the arithmetic of number fields.
